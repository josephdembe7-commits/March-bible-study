I — The Man Behind the Quill

A Traitor in the Eyes of His People

His name was Levi — the son of Alphaeus — and he sat where no respectable Jew would dare sit: at a tax booth along the dusty roads of Capernaum, collecting money for the Roman Empire that had crushed his nation underfoot.

To understand Matthew, you must first feel the weight of what he was. Tax collectors in first-century Judea were not simply civil servants. They were collaborators. They wore the shame of working for the very oppressor that taxed their own brothers and sisters without mercy — and many enriched themselves further by overcharging beyond what Rome required. In Jewish society, they were ranked alongside sinners and outcasts. To eat with a tax collector was scandal. To become one was near betrayal.

I
The Encounter

Imagine the scene: the midday heat of Galilee, the clink of coins, the averted eyes of passing neighbours. And then — a figure approaches. Not a Roman soldier. Not a Pharisee. But Jesus of Nazareth, who had just healed a paralyzed man and drawn crowds wherever He walked.

"Follow Me."

— Matthew 9:9, NKJV

Two words. And without hesitation, Matthew rose, left everything — the coins, the ledgers, the security of Roman employment — and followed. He who had spent his life counting what others owed now walked with the One who would cancel the debt of the world.

II — Twelve Among Many

Three Years at the Side of Christ

Matthew became one of Jesus' twelve chosen disciples — a quiet but diligent presence. Unlike Peter, who spoke impulsively, or John, who rested on Jesus' breast, Matthew's character was that of an observer. A recorder. A man trained by profession to notice detail, to write things down, to keep accounts. And now those very skills would be turned toward something eternal.

He walked the roads of Galilee, Judea, and beyond. He heard the Sermon on the Mount delivered in its fullness — those breathtaking Beatitudes that turned the world's values upside down. He watched lepers cleansed, storms silenced, the dead raised. He was there when five thousand were fed with a boy's lunch. He stood, trembling, in the upper room the night Jesus was betrayed.

As a former tax collector, Matthew would have been trilingual — fluent in Aramaic, Greek, and Latin. He was trained in meticulous record-keeping. God chose not to discard Matthew's past, but to consecrate it — turning the skills of a tax table into the instruments of Scripture.

He was present at the Resurrection appearances in Galilee. He was among those gathered in the upper room, praying with Mary and the apostles, when the Holy Spirit descended at Pentecost. And when the disciples scattered across the known world to preach — Matthew went too.

III — The Writing of the Word

Why He Wrote — and Who He Wrote For

The Gospel of Matthew was not written for everyone in the same way. Matthew knew his audience: Jewish believers who needed to see that the Messiah they had long awaited had finally come. More than any other Gospel writer, Matthew anchors the story of Jesus in the thread of Old Testament prophecy. Nearly every chapter rings with the refrain — "that it might be fulfilled which was spoken by the prophets."

Early church historians record that Matthew originally composed his Gospel in Hebrew — for his own people — before it was translated into Greek for wider circulation. The great church father Papias, writing around AD 110, confirmed: Matthew recorded the oracles of the Lord in the Hebrew tongue. Origen, Irenaeus, and others affirmed the same.

Matthew wrote his Gospel for Jewish people who had become followers of Christ. He wanted his audience to know that Jesus was the Messiah that God had promised — the fulfillment of everything spoken by the prophets.

— AskAnAdventistFriend.com

That is why when you open the Gospel of Matthew, the very first verse reads like a legal document: "The book of the genealogy of Jesus Christ, the Son of David, the Son of Abraham." This is Matthew speaking as the careful record-keeper he always was — building a case, laying out evidence, inviting the reader to conclude: This is the One. He has come. Everything you hoped for is here.

II
The Mission Beyond

Ethiopia, Persia, and Beyond

After preaching to the Hebrew people for approximately fifteen years, Matthew obeyed the Great Commission he had personally received from the risen Christ and carried the Gospel beyond Judea. Ancient church tradition places him in Ethiopia and Persia, where he is said to have established some of the earliest Christian communities on the African continent.

He did not write from comfort or distance. He wrote as one who had walked the roads, eaten the meals, wept the tears. And ultimately — in a land far from Capernaum — Matthew is believed to have died a martyr's death. The Roman Martyrology simply records: "Matthew, who preaching in Ethiopia, suffered martyrdom."

IV — Why This Matters to Us

Reading With New Eyes

Now that we know who Matthew was, we do not simply read his Gospel — we hear it differently. We hear a man who once cheated and collaborated and was despised, transformed by two words from Jesus into one of the most important writers in all of human history.

We hear someone who had every reason to be ashamed of his past, and who instead allowed that past to be redeemed — whose very skills of observation and record-keeping became the vessel through which God preserved the life of His Son for all generations.

When Matthew writes "Follow Me" — those two words that changed his life — he is not simply reporting history. He is extending the same invitation to every reader who has ever felt unworthy of grace. Matthew's entire Gospel is a testimony: If Jesus called me, He can call anyone.

This March, as we journey chapter by chapter through his 28 chapters — from the genealogy to the Great Commission — we carry with us the knowledge of the man behind the ink. We know his hands. We know his story. And that changes everything about how we receive his words.
